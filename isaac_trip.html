A 17-year-old has done a stage at Vanderlyle in Cambridge. He has stood next to a chef plating tasting menus. He knows more about food than most adults, including his Dad.

In this series, a father takes him to three more of those kitchens. We are looking at universities along the way — Imperial, Oxford, Sheffield — but the cathedrals here are the kitchens, and the universities are scenery.

Two men, a car, restaurants in England. Coogan and Brydon did the north and got six episodes out of it. We have three days and a son who already knows more about service than the father conducting the tour.

Episode One · Thursday
Pompette
Summertown, Oxford
"A French bistro in Summertown. The chef has, on the record, named Steve Coogan as a fantasy dinner-party guest. The series, in this small respect, casts itself."
Episode 1 · Pompette · Summertown

Pompette opened in Summertown in 2018 and has quietly become the best restaurant in Oxford. Pascal Wiedemann — chef and co-owner with his wife Laura — trained at Racine in Knightsbridge under Henry Harris, then opened Terroirs in Covent Garden, then ran Six Portland Road. When he serves you fish soup with rouille, or steak haché with a meaty jus, he is doing it from inside the French canon, not approximating it. The room is small, the wine list is excellent, the cooking is the unflashy kind that takes a decade to become good at.

One further detail you should know. In a recent interview Wiedemann was asked who he would invite to a dream dinner party. The list — Simon Hopkinson, Bruce Springsteen, Bob Mortimer, Ainsley Harriott, Andrew Flintoff — included Steve Coogan. The chef has, in writing, identified the host of this series as one of his fantasy guests. We are, evidently, in good hands.

The Ask

Mention Vanderlyle when you book. Wiedemann is the kind of chef who comes out to talk to a young cook about technique. Ask if he has five minutes between services. Most likely outcome: more than five minutes.

Episode Two · Friday
St John
Smithfield, London
Featuring Rhys
"A market lunch, eaten across seven stalls. An afternoon in the King's Cross AI quarter, gazing at the outside of buildings doing very consequential things. By evening, a converted smokehouse and a 1994 menu that has aged better than its diners."
Episode 2 · St John · Smithfield

Fergus Henderson opened St John in 1994 in a converted smokehouse next to Smithfield Market. The menu, in its broad shape, has not changed since. Roasted bone marrow with parsley salad. The chop. The Eccles cake. Henderson invented modern British cooking by deciding that the parts of animals nobody else wanted were, in fact, the parts worth eating — and that a dining room did not need to look like a dining room to be a serious one.

Half of Britain's most interesting kitchens trace through this room. James Lowe came up here before opening Lyle's. Isaac McHale before The Clove Club. Tom Harris, Anna Tobias, Margot Henderson — the modern British scene as we currently understand it is, more or less, a diaspora of this one smokehouse.

For this episode we are joined by Rhys, who has been cooking with Isaac since well before Isaac could reach the hob unassisted. Between them they have a kitchen vocabulary that goes back about a decade — chopping, timing, prepping, plating. Rhys is much closer to the start of the story than anyone.

St John is famous as a lunch place — bone marrow at noon, a pint with the chop, an Eccles cake while the dining room thins out. This trip will do dinner instead. Partly because the meal deserves the evening pace, partly because Smithfield at night, when the lights of the great Victorian market hall come on across the road and the meat porters arrive for their shift, is one of London's most atmospheric quarters. The bone marrow tastes the same at 8pm as it does at 1pm. The walk home, considerably better.

The Ask

Ask for a table at the bar at the front rather than the upstairs dining room — closer to the kitchen, less formal, same food. Order the bone marrow first, the chop second, and the Eccles cake whatever happens.

Elsewhere, this Episode
Morning2 hours

Imperial College, South Kensington

The reason the trip is happening. Imperial sits in South Kensington — flanked by the Natural History Museum and the V&A, one of the densest STEM ecosystems on the planet. Fifteen Nobel laureates. Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin in its medical school. Dennis Gabor invented holography in its physics department. Brian May completed his astrophysics PhD here, thirty years after he started it. The labs you can't see today are working on the next things.

Lunch90 mins

Borough Market — lunch on the hoof

Friday is the sweet spot — open as usual, with significantly less of the weekend tourist scrum. Graze. Oysters at Richard Haward's, pasta at Padella (or Flor across the road if the Padella queue is silly), jamón at Brindisa, a Comté tasting at Neal's Yard, a Monmouth espresso afterwards. Seven small meals from seven different stalls. The platonic London market lunch.

Afternoon2 hours

King's Cross — where the AI lives

Tube north from Borough. King's Cross has, in the past few years, quietly become the centre of British AI. Platform 37, Google's new Heatherwick + Bjarke Ingels building, is DeepMind's new home — named for the move AlphaGo made against Lee Sedol in 2016. Its ground floor will house The AI Exchange, a free public AI gallery (opens 2026 — check before you go). The British Library two minutes south is home to the Alan Turing Institute, the UK's national institute for AI. Walk Heatherwick's Coal Drops Yard, get a coffee in Granary Square. London's AI scene as a piece of architecture. One stop on the Met to Farringdon for dinner.

Episode Three · Saturday
JÖRO
Oughtibridge, Sheffield
"The final episode, in which the son returns to a kitchen that looks suspiciously like the one he just left, and the father realises that this was, perhaps, the point all along."
Episode 3 · JÖRO · The Tasting Menu

Luke French moved JÖRO out of its Kelham Island shipping container in 2024 into a new building at Oughtibridge — rooms attached, a more ambitious format, an explicit hospitality vision. The House of JÖRO. Which is, not incidentally, what Alex Rushmer is also reportedly thinking about with Vanderlyle's next chapter.

The plant-forward tasting menu, the Asian-influenced techniques, the open kitchen at the centre of everything — JÖRO is, of all the restaurants on this trip, the kitchen that most resembles the one Isaac left a few months ago. Same scale, same ambition, similar age cohort behind the pass, similar sensibility. One Michelin star, in a city most people don't know has a fine-dining scene.

The Ask — the big one

Email Luke French in advance. Stage at Vanderlyle, looking at Sheffield, would love to see the kitchen. Best case: he says yes to a brief pre-service walk-through, or even a couple of hours of prep on Saturday morning. Worst case: he says no, and you have a fantastic dinner anyway. Either way, the email is the move.

Notes on the Cast

Three kitchens, in context.

Pompette

Pascal Wiedemann trained at Racine in Knightsbridge under Henry Harris. Harris is now back in the kitchen at Bouchon Racine, above a 300-year-old Farringdon pub — possibly the hardest table to book in London. The teacher and the student are both in service. We are, by happy accident, eating the whole school.

St John

Fergus Henderson's Nose to Tail Eating (1999) sits on the shelf in any serious kitchen in Britain — including the one you have just worked in. Anthony Bourdain called St John his favourite restaurant in the world. More than once. The menu has barely changed in thirty years.

JÖRO

JÖRO opened in a shipping container in Kelham Island in 2016. It won its Michelin star in the shipping container. In 2024 it moved into House of JÖRO at Oughtibridge — proper restaurant, nine rooms attached, the format properly evolved. This is, not incidentally, the trajectory Alex Rushmer has hinted at for what comes next at Vanderlyle. You are seeing the future of the kitchen you have just worked in.

The Throughline

Three kitchens, three decades, three sizes. A Michelin-listed bistro (Pompette, 2018), the founding text of modern British cooking (St John, 1994), a rising one-star (JÖRO, 2016). None of them does what the others do. None of them can be properly understood without the other two.

What is the Trip?

The Trip is a BBC series (2010–2020) starring Steve Coogan and Rob Brydon playing fictionalised versions of themselves. The premise: they tour the country eating in restaurants, ostensibly on a newspaper assignment, while bickering, doing Michael Caine impressions, and reflecting on ageing and ambition between courses.

Four series, four countries, directed by Michael Winterbottom. The food is real, the restaurants chosen well. The conversations, very much improvised.

Watch the first series. Six episodes. Coogan is divorced and miserable, Brydon is contentedly married, they drive between restaurants in the north of England, and by the end very little has happened. Which is the point.
