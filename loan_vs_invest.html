Pay Now vs Take Loan & Invest

Blended portfolio return

Should you pay tuition upfront or take the student loan?

If you have the cash to pay university fees and living costs outright, you face a choice: spend it now and avoid the loan entirely, or take the maximum student loan and invest the money instead. This model compares both strategies over the 40-year life of a Plan 5 student loan.

The answer depends on three things: how much the graduate earns (which determines loan repayments), how the invested money grows (which depends on asset allocation and market returns), and crucially, whether the loan is ever fully repaid. Under Plan 5, any outstanding balance is written off after 40 years — so lower and moderate earners effectively receive a subsidy, making the loan cheaper than paying upfront regardless of investment returns.

Adjust the sliders to model different scenarios. The Monte Carlo simulation runs hundreds of paths with randomised annual returns to show the range of outcomes, not just the average.

How to read this model

This model compares two strategies for funding university. "Pay now" spends the full cost upfront — fees plus living expenses — leaving nothing invested. Net wealth is permanently £0. "Take loan + invest" borrows everything via the student loan and invests the equivalent cash in a portfolio. The graduate then repays the loan from salary over up to 40 years while the investment pot compounds.

The chart shows net wealth for the invest strategy: the investment pot minus cumulative loan repayments to date. Whenever the line is above £0, the invest strategy is ahead of pay now. The shaded bands show the Monte Carlo distribution — the range of outcomes from hundreds of simulated paths with randomised annual returns, capturing the uncertainty that real markets bring.

The write-off effect

Under Plan 5, any outstanding loan balance is written off after 40 years. For graduates who don't earn enough to repay in full, this means the total amount repaid is less than the original loan — the government absorbs the difference. This write-off acts as a subsidy that makes the loan strictly cheaper than paying upfront, even before considering any investment returns. Check the "Loan Outcome" KPI to see whether the loan is repaid or written off at your assumed salary.

The compounding effect

The invested lump sum compounds for the full 40 years. Even after subtracting all loan repayments, the remaining pot can grow substantially — especially with equity-heavy allocations over long horizons. The "P(Invest Wins)" KPI shows the probability that the investment pot exceeds total loan repayments at year 40 across all Monte Carlo simulations.

Why salary matters most

Loan repayments are 9% of income above the threshold. A graduate earning £30k repays very little; a graduate earning £80k repays aggressively. Higher earners repay the full loan plus RPI interest — potentially more than the original cost. But even then, the invested pot has had decades to compound, so investing often still wins. Move the salary slider to see how dramatically this changes the outcome.

What the model doesn't capture

This is a simplified illustration. It doesn't model: tax on investment returns (use an ISA to shelter the pot), platform charges or fund fees, the risk that the invested money gets spent before the 40 years are up, potential future changes to loan terms by government, or the psychological burden of carrying a large loan balance. The Monte Carlo assumes normally distributed returns — real markets have fatter tails.

Plan 5 loan: 9% above threshold, RPI + 0% interest, 40-year term, written off after 40 years. Monte Carlo uses normally distributed annual returns. No charges, CGT, or wrapper structure modelled. For illustration only.
