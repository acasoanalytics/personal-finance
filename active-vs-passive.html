The Arithmetic of Active Management

William Sharpe's 1991 insight is as elegant as it is devastating: before costs, the return of the average actively managed pound must equal the return of the average passively managed pound. Both, in aggregate, hold "the market." After costs, the average actively managed pound must therefore underperform the average passively managed pound — by precisely the amount of those costs.

This isn't an opinion or a statistical tendency. It's arithmetic. The only way an active manager can outperform is if another active manager underperforms by the same amount. It's a zero-sum game before fees and a negative-sum game after them. The fee differential between active and passive is the guaranteed headwind that active investors face every single year.

"After costs, the return on the average actively managed dollar will be less than the return on the average passively managed dollar for any time period." — William F. Sharpe, The Arithmetic of Active Management, 1991

The SPIVA Scorecards: Two Decades of Evidence

S&P Dow Jones Indices publishes the SPIVA (S&P Indices Versus Active) scorecards semi-annually, comparing actively managed funds against their benchmarks across every major market. The results are remarkably consistent across geographies and time periods:

92%
of UK equity funds underperformed the S&P United Kingdom BMI over 15 years (SPIVA Europe Year-End 2023)

The pattern holds globally. Over 15-year periods ending 2023:

  • US Large Cap: ~93% of active funds underperformed the S&P 500
  • Europe Equity: ~89% underperformed the S&P Europe 350
  • Emerging Markets: ~90% underperformed the S&P/IFCI Composite
  • Global Equity: ~88% underperformed the S&P Global 1200

Crucially, SPIVA corrects for survivorship bias — funds that close or merge (usually due to poor performance) are still counted in the data. Without this correction, active management's track record looks even worse than these figures suggest.

Persistence: Can You Just Pick the Good Ones?

The natural rejoinder is: "Fine, the average fund underperforms, but I'll just pick the top managers." The data on persistence makes this strategy deeply unreliable.

The S&P Persistence Scorecard examines whether top-quartile funds remain top-quartile in subsequent periods. The results are sobering: of US equity funds in the top quartile over any given five-year period, only about 3–5% remain in the top quartile for the subsequent five years. This is roughly consistent with chance.

The FCA's 2014 study of UK funds reached a similar conclusion: past performance offered essentially no predictive value for future returns once you controlled for fees and risk factors. The small number of persistent outperformers was statistically indistinguishable from what you'd expect by luck alone.

Star managers do exist — but identifying them in advance is the problem. You're much more likely to be paying premium fees for a manager who was lucky than one who is genuinely skilled. And even genuinely skilled managers face capacity constraints: as assets under management grow, the opportunity set for generating alpha shrinks.

The Compounding Tax: Why Small Fee Differences Create Huge Gaps

A 0.70% annual fee difference might seem trivial. But fees compound against you just as returns compound for you. The fee is levied on the entire pot every year — including on all the growth that previous years generated. Over long horizons, this compounding creates a staggering drag.

Adjust the sliders above to see this in action. At typical parameters (£100k initial, £500/mo contributions, 7% gross return, 25-year horizon), the difference between 0.15% and 0.85% OCF typically costs tens of thousands of pounds — money that would otherwise be compounding in your pot.

Warren Buffett put a number on it in his 2016 letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders: over the prior 50 years, he estimated that investors paying "helpers" (active managers, hedge funds, fund-of-funds) had wasted over $100 billion in aggregate. His advice? A low-cost index fund.

The Fee Share of Total Gain

One of the most powerful ways to see the cost of active management is to express fees not as a percentage of assets (the OCF), but as a percentage of the total investment gain. An 0.85% OCF sounds small. But when gross returns are 7%, that 0.85% represents over 12% of your annual growth being handed to the fund manager — every year, whether they beat the market or not.

Over a multi-decade horizon, cumulative fees can consume 20–30% or more of the total growth your money would have generated. The waterfall tab above shows this decomposition for your specific parameters.

With a passive fund at 0.15%, you keep the vast majority of market returns. The difference in the "fee share of gain" between passive and active is the true measure of what active management costs you — expressed not in obscure basis points, but in pounds you'll never compound.

What About "Cheap" Active Funds?

Some active funds have reduced their OCFs to 0.40–0.60% in response to fee pressure from passive competitors. This is positive but doesn't change the fundamental arithmetic: the median active fund still underperforms after any level of fee. A lower-cost active fund simply loses by less.

Morningstar's research has consistently found that the single best predictor of future fund performance is the expense ratio — not star ratings, not past returns, not the fund manager's pedigree. Low fees don't guarantee outperformance, but high fees virtually guarantee underperformance over long horizons.

If you do choose to allocate a satellite portion to active management, fee-consciousness is the most evidence-based selection criterion available. But for the core of a portfolio, the case for passive is overwhelming.
