Model Guide

The problem

You have spent decades building a pot of savings. Now you need to turn it into an income — and nobody will tell you what markets do next. Spend too freely and a bad decade can leave you short when you are least able to recover. Spend too cautiously and you spend your one retirement denying yourself things you could easily have afforded. Almost everyone errs on the second side, which feels safe but is a real loss too: it is holidays not taken and help not given, in exchange for dying with a large bank balance.

Why the order of returns matters so much

Here is the part that makes this genuinely hard. Two retirees can earn exactly the same average return over thirty years and end up in wildly different places, purely because the good and bad years arrived in a different order. A crash in your first five years of retirement, while you are selling investments every month to pay for life, does lasting damage — each sale during the crash locks the loss in permanently. The identical crash at age eighty barely matters. So the early years are a danger window, and any sensible plan should treat them differently.

The old answer, and its flaw

The famous rule of thumb says: withdraw 4% of your starting pot in year one, increase it with inflation each year, and never look at your balance again. It survives most of history. But notice what it refuses to do: respond to information. It must be cautious enough for the worst histories, which means in the ordinary ones it systematically underspends — the typical follower of the rule dies with roughly the pot they started with. That leftover money is the price of ignoring how things are actually going.

What this tool does instead

You give it five things: your pot, the minimum income you genuinely need each year (the floor), the most you would actually enjoy spending (the ceiling), how many years the money must last, and how much weight the worst outcomes should carry. The solver then works out the best possible response to every situation you could find yourself in — for each combination of “this much wealth” and “this many years left”, the best amount to withdraw and the best split between shares and bonds. It is not a rule of thumb; it is the mathematically best plan for the assumptions you feed it, taken from a 2021 paper by the actuarial researcher Peter Forsyth.

What the best plan turns out to look like

Two findings, both a little surprising. First, spending: the best plan is all-or-nothing. Take only the floor while your future security is still in doubt — typically the first five to ten years, the danger window — then jump to the ceiling once wealth has pulled comfortably ahead, and enjoy it. Amounts in between are almost never worth taking. The early restraint is not timidity; it is precisely what pays for the much higher income later.

Second, investing: hold more in shares after markets fall, and wind the risk right down once you are safely ahead of target. That sounds backwards, but the logic is clean. If you are already certain of meeting your goal, taking more risk gains you nothing you value. If you have fallen behind, cautious investments cannot close the gap. So safety is something the plan earns by getting ahead — not something scheduled by your birthday, as conventional “de-risk with age” funds do.

How the tool keeps score on risk

Rather than asking “what is the chance of running out?”, it asks a sharper question: when things go badly, how badly do they go? The headline risk number is the average ending position across the worst 1-in-20 outcomes. A negative figure does not mean bankruptcy — it means those unlucky versions of you would need to lean on resources outside the pot, such as housing wealth. The dial called κ sets how heavily those worst cases count against everyday income. There is no correct setting: solve, look at the worst-case number, and adjust until it is one you could genuinely live with.

Reading the screens

The wealth fan shows the range of futures — the band covers most of what could happen, the dark line is the middle path. The withdrawals tab shows the income pattern: floor early, ceiling later. The control maps are the plan itself — find your wealth and the year, and the colour tells you what to do. The comparison tab pits the optimal plan against simpler rules on identical simulated markets, including a “worst-case income” column, because some rules that look safe merely hide their risk in your grocery budget rather than your estate.

The small print

Everything is in today’s money, so figures need no inflation adjustment in your head. Tax, fees and account types are not modelled — use this to understand the shape of a good plan, not to set a budget. The standard market assumptions come from a century of American history, which may flatter the future; it is worth re-solving with a lower share return to see how the plan holds up. And the horizon is fixed rather than a lifespan — real planning should leave room for living longer than you budgeted for.

What this tool does
You supply four things: a pot, the minimum yearly income you need (the floor), the maximum you would actually enjoy spending (the ceiling), and a time horizon. The solver then works out, for every possible combination of wealth and year, the best amount to withdraw and the best equity/bond split. “Best” means: the highest average lifetime income that still keeps the worst outcomes within bounds. It is not a rule of thumb — it is the mathematically optimal plan for the market assumptions you give it, from a 2021 actuarial paper by Peter Forsyth.
How risk is measured
Rather than asking “what is the chance of running out?”, the tool asks “when things go badly, how badly do they go?” The headline risk number (ES, expected shortfall) is the average ending position across the worst 1-in-20 outcomes. Two plans can have the same chance of trouble while one leaves you slightly short and the other severely short — this measure tells them apart. The κ dial sets how heavily those worst cases weigh against average income when the solver makes its trade-offs.
The withdrawal rule it finds
The optimal plan is all-or-nothing: withdraw only the floor while your future security is in doubt, then the full ceiling once wealth is comfortably ahead — amounts in between are almost never best. On a typical path that means floor for the first five to ten years, ceiling thereafter. The early restraint is the whole trick: a poor run of markets does far more damage at the start of retirement, while you are selling assets to live on, than the same run does later. Spending lightly through that danger window is what buys the higher income afterwards.
The investment rule it finds
The equity rule may look backwards at first: hold more equity after markets fall, and cut it right down once you are safely ahead of target. The logic: if you are already sure of meeting your goal, further risk gains you nothing worth having; if you have fallen behind, bond returns alone cannot close the gap. Compare this with a fixed 60/40 mix or a conventional “de-risk with age” glidepath — here, de-risking is something you earn by getting ahead, not something scheduled by birthday.
Why it beats a fixed 4%-style rule
A fixed inflation-linked withdrawal ignores how your investments actually perform. To survive the worst histories it must underspend in most of them — look at the constant rule’s typical leftover pot in the comparison tab, often as large as the pot you started with. That is money you earned, saved and never spent. Letting withdrawals respond to actual wealth harvests the good outcomes: in the paper’s tests, roughly a third more average income at essentially the same level of worst-case risk. All benchmarks here are run on identical simulated markets, so the differences are pure strategy — including a version of the dynamic-amortisation (ARVA/PMT) rule clamped to the same floor and ceiling, so you can see exactly what the optimisation adds over a sensible mechanical rule.
Limits & small print
Everything is in today’s money — withdrawals are assumed to rise with inflation automatically. No tax, fees, or account types are modelled: use it to understand the shape of a good plan, not to compute an after-tax budget. The default market assumptions come from US history 1926–2019, which may flatter the future — it is worth re-solving with a lower equity return. A plan ending “below zero” here means leaning on resources outside the pot (home equity, downsizing), not bankruptcy. The horizon is fixed: it does not model an uncertain lifespan. Finally, if the ceiling is tiny relative to the pot (a 1–2% withdrawal rate), the model’s trade-off quietly becomes spending versus worst-case estate size — it will hold the floor early whenever κ > 1 because a withheld pound compounds into the worst-case leftover at κ times the value of a spent one. The tool flags this regime in the comparison tab.
Implements the pre-commitment EW–ES objective of Forsyth (2021), "A Stochastic Control Approach to Defined Contribution Plan Decumulation", North American Actuarial Journal 26(2) 227–251 (arXiv:2008.06598), via 1-D wealth-grid dynamic programming (valid by the paper's Prop. 6.1: optimal controls depend on total wealth only), stratified-quadrature transition expectations, and outer golden-section search over the embedded VaR level W*. Engine validated against the paper: constant-strategy ES/medians (Table 10.1) within ~1%; constant-withdrawal optimal-allocation case ES −198.2 vs published −196.1 (Table 11.2); at the paper's (κ, W*) the published frontier point is reproduced (EW 54.37 vs 54.32, ES −213.7 vs −209.5, Table A.2). The κ → frontier-point mapping is ill-conditioned where the frontier is flat (noted in the paper), so judge strategies by realised ES/EW, not by κ itself. Everything is in real terms; insolvent wealth accrues at the bond process (implicit borrowing capacity, e.g. housing equity). No tax, fees, or wrapper structure modelled. Pre-commitment ES = induced time-consistent target-shortfall strategy (implementable). For illustration only — not advice.
