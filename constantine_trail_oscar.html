You're walking between two empires.

Four days in northern England, then Istanbul. The same emperor connects them — and you're going to stand at both ends of his story. Plus a stop at Durham along the way to see what student life looks like in a Norman castle.

1,720years between the spot you'll stand on in York and today
The Big Idea

An emperor was made on Yorkshire mud. He went on to invent a city we're about to visit.

In 306 AD, the Roman Emperor Constantius died at York while campaigning. His soldiers immediately turned to his son Constantine and proclaimed him emperor on the spot. Constantine went on to defeat his rivals, convert to Christianity, and found a brand new capital on the Bosphorus.

He called it Constantinople. We call it Istanbul. The city you'll visit was built because of what happened in a Yorkshire fortress 1,720 years ago — and you're going to stand on the spot where it started.

Day 1

York — Where an Emperor Was Made

Start in the city the Romans called Eboracum. The bronze statue of Constantine outside York Minster marks roughly the spot of his acclamation. Beneath the cathedral lie the foundations of the Roman fortress headquarters — the principia where it all happened.

9:30 AM2 hours

York Minster — Constantine statue & Roman undercroft

The bronze Constantine sits outside the south transept. Then go inside and down to the undercroft — you can see the foundations of the Roman fortress headquarters directly beneath the cathedral.

Look out forThe exact paving stone level of the Roman building, four metres below the modern floor. You're standing one storey down from where they made him emperor.
11:45 AM30 mins

Multangular Tower

Five minutes' walk from the Minster, in Museum Gardens. One of eight defensive towers from the Roman legionary fortress, and the only one still standing.

Look out forThe change in the stonework. The lower 6 metres of small stones are genuine 3rd-century Roman. The bigger blocks above are medieval. You can see exactly where Rome ended.
12:30 PM2 hours

Yorkshire Museum

Strong Roman gallery covering the Eboracum garrison. Closed Mondays. Lunch in the Museum Gardens café.

Look out forThe carved stone heads of Roman gods worshipped by the legion at York — the legion that would, twenty years later, march south with Constantine and reshape an empire.
3:00 PM75 mins · optional

JORVIK Viking Centre

Different era, but tells the story of what happened to York after Rome. Worth booking ahead. If you'd rather, skip this and head north to Durham early — the riverside walk in evening light is excellent.

Day 2

Durham — A Cathedral on a Rock, and the Man Who Wrote England's First History

Drive an hour up the A1 from York. The peninsula site — the River Wear curving around three sides, cathedral and castle on the high point — is one of the most dramatic urban landscapes in Britain. This is also your first proper look at a possible university. Durham is collegiate: students live and dine in colleges scattered across the medieval city, several of them inside the castle itself.

10:00 AM90 mins

Durham Cathedral

Norman cathedral, completed 1133 AD. One of the greatest Romanesque buildings in Europe — the massive piers in the nave are unlike anything else. Inside lie two of the most important figures in early English Christianity: St Cuthbert and the Venerable Bede.

The Constantine connectionBede's Ecclesiastical History of the English People (731 AD) is the first proper history of England. He explicitly modelled it on Eusebius's Life of Constantine. Bede's tomb is in the Galilee Chapel at the west end. You're standing over the bones of the man who carried the Constantinople tradition of historical writing to Britain. That thread you've been following just got 425 years more interesting.
11:45 AM60 mins

Durham Castle & University College

The castle has been continuously inhabited for nearly a thousand years and is now home to University College — students literally live in a Norman castle. Tours run when undergraduates aren't using it (check timing on the day). Even just walking around Palace Green between cathedral and castle gives you the feel of the place.

Look out forThe Norman Chapel beneath the keep — built in 1078, the oldest surviving building in Durham. And imagine where your dorm room might be.
1:00 PM90 mins

Lunch & the Bailey

Pick a spot on Saddler Street or North/South Bailey. The colleges spill into the streets here — Hatfield, St John's, St Chad's, Hild Bede. Get a sense of the everyday rhythm of student life.

2:45 PM60 mins

Riverside peninsula walk

Walk the loop along the Wear (about 1.5 miles, mostly flat). Gives you the cathedral framed from below — the view that convinced the Normans to pick this spot. Pass the college boathouses and Prebends Bridge.

Look out forThe Cathedral from the south, viewed across the river. This is one of the most photographed buildings in England, and you'll see why.
Day 3

Vindolanda — The Frontier Comes Alive

Drive west to Hexham (about 90 minutes from Durham). Vindolanda is one of the most actively excavated Roman sites in the world — they're still pulling things out of the ground every summer. The on-site museum holds the Vindolanda tablets: the oldest surviving handwritten documents in Britain.

11:00 AM3–4 hours

Vindolanda Roman Fort & Museum

Walk the excavated fort and vicus (civilian settlement), then the museum. April–September, you can usually watch active digs in progress. Buy the joint Vindolanda + Roman Army Museum ticket online for a discount.

Don't missTablet 291 — a birthday party invitation from one Roman officer's wife to another, written around 100 AD. The first known handwriting by a woman in Latin, anywhere in the world. It's tiny. You have to lean in.
3:30 PM90 mins

Roman Army Museum (Walltown)

15-minute drive west of Vindolanda. Smaller, more focused on the soldiers themselves: kit, weapons, daily life, plus the Eagle's Eye 3D film.

Look out forThe recruitment posters showing where the Hadrian's Wall garrisons were drawn from — Syrians, Spaniards, Germans, North Africans. The Roman Empire's frontier was wildly multicultural.
Day 4

Hadrian's Wall — Walking the Empire's Edge

Today you actually walk on the wall itself. Housesteads is the most complete fort in the most dramatic setting. Chesters has the best surviving Roman bath-house in Britain.

10:00 AM2.5 hours

Housesteads Roman Fort (Vercovicium)

Steep walk up from the car park, but the views over Northumberland are spectacular and you can walk west along the wall itself toward Sycamore Gap. Go early to beat the coaches.

Look out forThe latrines. Genuinely. They're the most complete Roman toilet block in Britain — 24 men sat in a row over a continuously flushing channel of water, with a separate channel in front for hand-washing sponges. Roman plumbing, fully visible, 1,900 years on.
12:30 PM1 hour · optional

Sycamore Gap walk

About a mile's walk west of Housesteads along the wall. The famous tree was felled in 2023 but the gap and the wall stretch are still one of the most photographed sections in Britain.

2:30 PM2 hours

Chesters Roman Fort & Museum

Cavalry fort with the best-preserved Roman bath-house in Britain — changing rooms, hot rooms, underfloor heating, all clearly visible.

Look out forThe arched doorway of the changing room — it still stands, 1,800 years later, exactly as Roman cavalrymen walked through it after a hard ride along the frontier. Touch it. There aren't many surfaces in Britain you can touch that they touched.
Genuinely cool things you'll see

The bits worth getting excited about.

Handwriting older than the New Testament's oldest copies

The Vindolanda tablets predate the earliest surviving Gospel manuscripts by over 100 years. Letters between officers, supply lists, a soldier's mother sending him socks for the British winter. Astonishing.

A Roman bath-house with the heating still visible

At Chesters, you can see the hypocaust — the underfloor heating system. The pillars that held up the floor, the channels the hot air ran through, the works. Roman engineering, intact.

The exact spot

Outside York Minster, on the south side, stands a bronze statue of Constantine looking like he just got the news. Stand next to it and read the plaque. This is real.

An active dig

If you go May–September, Vindolanda has volunteer archaeologists working live trenches. You can stand at the edge and watch them lift something out of the dirt that's been buried for 2,000 years.

Then — Istanbul.

And here's the bit that should give you chills.

When you walk into the Hagia Sophia later this year, look up at the dome. It's the largest church interior in the world for a thousand years. It was built by an emperor called Justinian, sitting on a throne that Constantine founded — the same Constantine who was made emperor on the ground we just walked over in York.

From a bronze statue in a Yorkshire cathedral close, to the largest dome in Christendom, in one continuous human story. You will have stood at both ends of it.
