The formula, the intuition, and the caveats

Robert Merton's 1969 result gives a closed-form answer to one of the oldest questions in finance: how much of your wealth should be in risky assets versus cash? The answer is surprisingly compact:

w* = (μ − r) / (γ · σ²) where w* is the optimal fraction in the risky asset, μ is its expected return, r is the risk-free rate, σ is its volatility, and γ is the investor's coefficient of relative risk aversion.

The intuition is clean. The numerator (μ − r) is the reward — how much extra return you get for taking risk. The denominator γ · σ² is the cost — variance is what hurts you, scaled by how much you personally dislike that hurt. Allocate more when the reward is large, less when variance or your aversion to it is large. Everything else in finance theory descends from this trade-off.

A useful reformulation: w* = SR / (γ · σ), where SR is the Sharpe ratio. This makes a point worth absorbing — two assets with the same Sharpe ratio get the same dollar allocation regardless of their volatility, because in the Merton framework you scale your exposure to match your appetite, not the asset's own riskiness. Volatility only matters as a scaling factor for how much capital you commit.

The certainty-equivalent return at the optimum has its own elegant form: CE* = r + SR² / (2γ). The excess CE over the risk-free rate scales with the square of the Sharpe ratio — doubling the Sharpe quadruples the value you extract — and inversely with risk aversion. A modest improvement in Sharpe is worth more than a modest reduction in volatility at the same Sharpe.

What γ actually is. The coefficient of relative risk aversion is the elasticity of marginal utility with respect to wealth. It's not a number you can observe directly — it has to be calibrated to behaviour. Some rough anchors:

γ = 1 (log utility)
The Kelly criterion. Maximises geometric growth rate. Often considered too aggressive for real investors — it tolerates very deep drawdowns.
γ = 2–4
Typical range inferred from real portfolio choices, surveys, and macro asset-pricing models. γ = 3 roughly fits a 60/40 portfolio at historical parameters.
γ = 5–10
Conservative or wealthy investors prioritising capital preservation. Used in retirement glide-paths and endowment frameworks.
γ > 10
Pathologically risk-averse in pure theory, though sometimes needed to fit observed equity premium puzzles. Suggests something other than CRRA is going on.

Leverage and short positions. The formula doesn't impose w ∈ [0, 1]. With a high Sharpe ratio and low γ, the optimal allocation can exceed 100% — meaning borrow at r to invest more than your wealth in the risky asset. With negative excess return, optimal w is negative — short the risky asset. Real-world borrowing is rarely at the risk-free rate, and shorting carries costs and risks the formula doesn't capture, so treat extreme allocations as theoretical upper bounds rather than literal prescriptions.

Five reasons to take the answer with a grain of salt:
  1. Parameter uncertainty. μ in particular is estimated with huge error. A standard error of 2% on expected equity returns is normal; that propagates to massive uncertainty in w*. The formula gives a point estimate as if you knew the inputs precisely; you don't.
  2. Single-period vs continuous. Merton's result assumes continuous trading with no costs. In practice, transaction costs and rebalancing frictions eat into the theoretical optimum.
  3. No regime change. The formula assumes constant μ, r, σ. Volatility clusters, returns mean-revert, and risk premia move. Dynamic strategies that respond to these can outperform the static Merton share.
  4. One risky asset. Real portfolios have many. The multi-asset version replaces σ² with a covariance matrix and (μ − r) with a vector — same structure, but diversification effects matter.
  5. CRRA is a model. Real preferences include loss aversion, habit formation, and aspiration levels that CRRA doesn't capture. The Merton share is the answer to a clean question, not necessarily the answer to your question.

Despite all this, the formula is the right starting point. It tells you what you'd do if you knew the world, gives you a benchmark to deviate from with reason rather than instinct, and forces every input to be made explicit. The instinct to "go 100% in stocks because they go up over time" is the instinct to set γ = 1 and ignore parameter uncertainty. The instinct to hold 100% cash is the instinct to set γ = ∞. Merton's formula doesn't tell you what γ to use — but it forces you to admit you're using one.
